"The ides of March are come."

- Caesar

Before this particular scene, Caesar was warned by the soothsayer about the ides of March. However, as triumphantly expressed above, Caesar believes that the ides of March are over. Which results in the soothsayer providing Caesar with an even more ambiguous response, that fate might still prevail.

In addition, Caesar is given another warning. Artemidorus urges Caesar to read his letter which contains the events that are bound to happen later. However, Caesar denies to read any letters pertaining to him as he believes he must prioritize other's welfare rather than his own.

Both of the denials by Caesar create a dramatic irony between the audience. As the audience is aware of the future assassination and the importance of the warnings. But, Caesar continues to ignore them which will eventually lead him to his own downfall.

"Et tu, Brute?"

This line stands out in many ways. Firstly, it is one of the only uses of latin in the play. Additionally, there is a very strong meaning behind it. “Et tu Brute” or “You too Brutus”. The phrase represents the moment when Caesar realizes that even his trusted friend, Brutus, has joined the conspirators in assassinating him. The line is a poignant representation of betrayal, friendship, and power, and it has become a popular phrase in modern culture to express a sense of betrayal or disappointment.

"And Caesar’s spirit, ranging for revenge,
With Ate by his side come hot from hell,
Shall in these confines with a monarch’s voice
Cry “Havoc!” and let slip the dogs of war,"

- Antony

Antony plays the key role in the rest of the tragedy. During the assassination, the conspirators made sure Antony was not there, so their plan would go accordingly. After the death of Caesar, Antony went to the capital to make peace with conspirators. He first asks if he is able to speak at the funeral. Brutus agrees as he thinks everyone will agree with his logic, however Cassius is doubtful as he is aware of Antony’s strong rhetorical appeal. Additionally, Antony has a soliloquy where he expresses his true intentions. As shown in the excerpt above, Caesar’s ghost will avenge his death. The allusion in the excerpt supports this idea as Ate is the ancient Greek goddess of destruction and folly, who is by Caesar’s “side”. The excerpt also foreshadows the upcoming war which most definitely includes destruction and folly.
